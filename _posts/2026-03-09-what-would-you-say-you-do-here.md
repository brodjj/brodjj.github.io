AI leaders have been making some pretty bold claims about job displacement lately. Given that the people making them have a vested interest in you believing them, they're worth scrutinising.

Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei warned at the World Economic Forum in Davos in January 2026 that AI could eliminate 50% of entry-level white-collar jobs within one to five years, potentially pushing unemployment to 10-20%. At the same event, he predicted AI would take over nearly all software engineering within 6 to 12 months. OpenAI's Sam Altman was slightly more measured, acknowledging that some companies are engaging in "AI washing" (blaming AI for layoffs that would have happened anyway) but predicting the real impact would become "palpable" within a few years. Microsoft's Satya Nadella, meanwhile, took the poetic route, arguing we should think of AI as "scaffolding for human potential" rather than a replacement, which is a lovely sentiment from a man whose company laid off 15,000 people in 2025 while posting record profits.

These claims caused me to have a mild panic attack when I first read them. It's certainly true that LLM and agentic AI capabilities are increasing at a rapid rate. Think about your own experience of messing around with ChatGPT 12 months ago vs. today. Early on, performance was sometimes mind-blowing, sometimes inane, and usually unreliable. If you attempted long or complex tasks in an early iteration of ChatGPT, it was a question of when, not if, the output would go off the rails with hallucinations.

These days, the capabilities seem a lot more stable. If you give most popular LLMs a quality prompt, you'll get usable output 9 times out of 10. But does this all of a sudden mean that organisations are going to dive in and replace workers at scale?

The short answer is, no one really knows. There are a bunch of factors, both human and technical, that will dictate how quickly AI will infiltrate the workforce at scale. The likes of Altman and Amodei would love for this to happen sooner rather than later, but of course CEOs of AI companies make these bold claims: their businesses currently rely on hype and hope. Hope that investing now (in AI companies that are still mostly cashflow negative) will pay off if they can ship profitable products and dominate the market.

Here's my attempt at a balanced analysis of the factors for and against rapid AI adoption in workplaces ("rapid" being more aggressive and complex than "our staff sometimes use Copilot to summarise documents or help write emails").

## Factors for rapid AI adoption by employers

### It's cheap

Compared to the cost of employing a person, AI is very affordable. A ChatGPT Enterprise licence costs roughly $60 per user per month. A graduate marketing coordinator in Sydney costs roughly $60,000 a year plus super. You don't need an MBA to do those maths.

In some functions that are perceived to have lower risk (a perception that will hold right up until the AI helpdesk calls your CEO a shrew, or the AI-generated internal newsletter accidentally announces a round of layoffs that hasn't happened yet), bringing in AI for productivity gains or even replacing some staff could be tempting for managers focused on cost-cutting, and willing to take the risk on outsourcing previously human-led functions to AI.

This may start with functions outside of an organisation. Maybe your company has been hiring a small marketing agency to write new copy for the website a couple of times a year. If an AI can do a comparable job for tens of dollars rather than thousands, that's the kind of easy win that gets axed first.

### FOMO

"Everyone's adopting AI! If we don't do it today, we're going to be left behind" will be the words many of you hear before a hasty and ill-advised AI rollout tanks your company's stock price.

It's not just middle managers getting twitchy. Boards are now routinely asking CEOs "what's our AI strategy?" at quarterly updates. Investors want to hear the magic words in earnings calls. If you're a publicly listed company and you haven't mentioned AI in your last annual report, you might as well have printed it on papyrus and delivered it by carrier pigeon. Nobody wants to be the CEO who has to explain to shareholders why their competitors are using AI and they're still doing things the old-fashioned way, even if "the old-fashioned way" is working perfectly fine.

However, FOMO may be justified for some. Businesses that get AI implementation right will be turning their profit amplifier up to 11 with significant efficiency and productivity gains. Strategic application of AI enhancements could allow a business to spend more time on revenue-generating activities (by empowering teams to take on more customers and deliver more products and services in shorter timeframes) and less time on activities that would normally soak up your productive hours: admin, reporting, back-office processing, and all the other boring but important stuff you've had to do to keep the lights on.

### Businesses looking to scale up their operations

Some businesses that are currently doing well may be poised to introduce AI into different aspects of their operations to scale to the next level.

If you run a sales-based business and your inbox is already running hot with leads from potential new clients, an AI might be able to help you prioritise which ones to respond to first based on an analysis of their sentiment ("keen and ready to buy" vs. "kicking tyres and wasting your time") and what the potential value of the sale is likely to be.

Translation and localisation AI may allow you to expand into foreign markets that would normally be inaccessible, or would require significant planning and investment to tap into.

Customer onboarding is another area where AI could help a growing business scale without proportionally scaling headcount. If you're a SaaS company that currently has three customer success managers walking new clients through setup and training, an AI-powered onboarding assistant could handle the routine walkthroughs and FAQ responses, freeing up your human team to focus on the complex or high-value accounts that actually benefit from a personal touch.

The common thread here is that AI isn't necessarily replacing people in these scenarios. It's handling the overflow that would otherwise require hiring, which is a much easier sell for most businesses than "let's fire Sharon and replace her with a chatbot."

## Factors against rapid AI adoption by employers

### Most business operations and workflows are complicated

Even highly successful, tech-forward businesses have weird, idiosyncratic systems and processes. "We're now 100% on Oracle! Except for when we deal with the Canada office... they're still on Salesforce. Oh, and if you need to export a ticket to the network vendor, make sure you run this script on it to add the right message header for their CRM..."

It seems tempting to think that maybe an AI agent could be put in place to navigate these disparate workflows and improve efficiency, until you realise that this would involve extensive training, configuring complex tool calls, configuring guardrails, and doing a shit-tonne of testing to make sure that the AI won't go off-script and take over your company. There are enough horror stories of AI agents leaking API keys or accidentally deleting things they're not supposed to (Samsung engineers infamously leaked proprietary source code via ChatGPT back in 2023, which is the kind of security incident that makes your CISO age ten years overnight) that any sane CTO has to weigh up the risk-to-reward ratio of keeping things as they are, attempting a middleware project with a vendor with a known track record, or rolling the dice on a bleeding-edge AI technology that may or may not try to send insider information to a data centre in China.

A human employee who's been in the role for six months already knows that you have to run that script before exporting the ticket, that the Canada office doesn't use Oracle, and that Dave in procurement gets funny about purchase orders over $500. Getting an AI agent to reliably navigate that kind of institutional knowledge is a problem that sounds simple and is anything but.

### Current AI tools aren't necessarily the right fit for many businesses

In many businesses, AI technology (at least with current capabilities) isn't directly applicable. Domestic and commercial trades are a key example. If you're an electrician who spends 7 hours a day wiring people's homes, AI isn't going to replace you or any of your employees doing the same kind of work. Even if sophisticated robots become affordable in 20 years, will they really be able to do all the nuanced problem-solving that the average electrician does before his or her first smoko break?

I once had an electrician at my house, running a fibre optic line from one corner of the property to the other. What started off as a simple job hit a literal snag when the cable he was trying to snake under the house got stuck on some hidden obstruction that didn't appear on the floor plan (the house is over 100 years old, so Christ knows what's under there). In the end, we could only get the cable where it needed to go by fixing a wire hook to one end, then communicating on our mobile phones while I stood at one corner of the house and pushed the cable through the conduit, and the electrician tried to fly-fish a hooked wire under the house to catch it and pull it through. Miraculously, we were able to pull off this crazy manoeuvre after only a few attempts. I don't see the next generation ASIMO being able to do that (not unless it has the engineering of an F-35 Joint Strike Fighter and the price tag to match).

> This is a well-known concept in AI research called Moravec's Paradox: tasks that are trivially easy for humans (navigating a cluttered room, catching a ball, improvising a solution with a bit of wire) turn out to be incredibly difficult for AI. Meanwhile, tasks that humans find hard (complex maths, searching millions of documents) are comparatively easy for machines. Your electrician's ability to improvise under a 100-year-old house is, paradoxically, one of the hardest things in the world to automate.

### Stakes are high

If things are working well for your business, do you really want to take the gamble on a complex AI integration? The most powerful and capable AI tools are the ones with the most potential to give you a good return on investment, but they also happen to be the ones with the greatest potential to damage your business.

The risks aren't just theoretical. A Meta AI security researcher recently reported that an AI agent [ran amok on her inbox](https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/23/a-meta-ai-security-researcher-said-an-openclaw-agent-ran-amok-on-her-inbox/), deleting and modifying emails without authorisation. Air Canada found itself legally liable after its customer service chatbot hallucinated a bereavement fare discount policy that didn't exist, and a court ruled the airline had to honour it. These aren't obscure edge cases from experimental labs. These are real businesses, deploying real AI tools, and getting real consequences.

Then there's the reputational risk. Internal failures are bad enough, but when your AI screws up publicly, the internet is not kind. One viral screenshot of your chatbot saying something unhinged can undo years of brand-building faster than you can draft an apology.

> I've written separately about why [AI systems should be held to the same safety standards as industrial equipment](/posts/ai-safety-applying-industrial-guardrails/). The short version: if your money-making tool hurts someone because you didn't test it properly, that's on you, whether the tool runs on hydraulics or neural networks.
{: .prompt-tip }

For many businesses, the rational calculation isn't "how quickly can we adopt AI?" but "how much do we stand to lose if this goes wrong?" And for a lot of companies, especially those in regulated industries or dealing with sensitive customer data, the answer to that second question is "quite a lot more than we'd save."

## Change happens slowly (or: a brief history of the future taking its time)

Change often happens more slowly than we think. If I reflect on my experience of the internet and the resulting digitisation of business and commerce, it took several years to really see things take off, and even then, not all organisations adopted it at any kind of scale (running your business on a giant Excel spreadsheet and communicating with your customers via a free Yahoo email doesn't count).

I was lucky enough to have a dial-up internet connection in my house towards the end of 1996. Lots of businesses and organisations had websites by around 1997/1998, but these were by no means high-powered e-commerce portals. They were static text and images, hand-coded in Notepad by some bored developer looking to capitalise on the dot-com boom, and provided little functionality beyond an About page, a phone number and an email address. For most businesses, the internet was effectively being used as a digital version of the Yellow Pages until the early to mid 2000s.

Digital systems inside businesses and organisations were even less promising. Online banking didn't really start until the late 1990s, and even that was pretty rudimentary. You could check your balance and perform basic transactions. If you needed to apply for a new account, credit card or loan, you still had to physically go into a branch and sign a paper form with a pen like a gold trader in the 1600s. This was the case until the mid 2000s. I'm surprised they still didn't make you use a wax seal.

Small to medium businesses were even further behind. During the mid to late 2000s I worked at a motorcycle workshop and we still managed job cards on an IBM AS/400 system that ran on a beige box PC under the counter. We had a separate, disconnected Windows NT terminal for internet, email and other back-office functions, and even that seemed like steam-engine era technology compared to the gaming PC I had at home. Online bookings and automated notifications were certainly possible with the tech of the day, but this would've required replacing the entire suite of expensive job management and back-office systems that would have been a large investment when they were originally purchased in the '90s. A business that's only barely cashflow positive after paying for staff, rent, utilities and other expenses isn't going to spend tens of thousands of dollars in the hopes of replacing the one guy who sits at the desk and deals with customers. Automated systems could maybe do 80% of that job. You still need a guy to upsell a K&N air filter, placate an irate customer about his repair bill, or warn someone to take it easy on a new set of tyres so they don't lose traction and dump their bike on the street as they leave the workshop.

Many businesses still operate this way in the 2020s. The combination of "if it ain't broke" and "the people we have in these roles actually help drive sales (or avoid costly complaints)" means that the incentives to replace large sections of your workforce with AI aren't as strong as they might seem on paper.

## Conclusion: The Bobs are coming, but they're still figuring out their game plan

So where does all this leave us?

AI leaders are predicting a "white-collar bloodbath." The data, so far, is telling a very different story. Yale Budget Lab research using Bureau of Labor Statistics data found no significant AI-related labour changes through November 2025. Deutsche Bank analysts have warned that "AI redundancy washing" will be a significant feature of 2026, as companies blame AI for cuts that have other causes. And Amodei's own prediction that 90% of code would be AI-written by the end of 2025? It turned out to be true for Anthropic itself, but at other software companies the figure was between 25% and 40%. The man building the technology has a skewed sense of how quickly everyone else can adopt it, which is a pattern we've seen before.

Here's what's interesting: a 2026 Vanguard economic forecast found that the roughly 100 occupations most exposed to AI automation are actually outperforming the rest of the labour market in terms of job growth and real wage increases. At first glance, that looks like good news. But if you were around for the dot-com boom, it might also look familiar. In the late 1990s, every business rushed to "go online." They hired webmasters, bought expensive CMS platforms, and invested in e-commerce portals that nobody used. For a while, the jobs and spending associated with the internet boom were booming too. Then the bubble burst, and it took another five to ten years of quiet, unglamorous iteration before the internet actually transformed how most businesses operated. The companies that thrived weren't the ones who jumped in first. They were the ones who figured out what the technology was actually good for, and implemented it at the right pace with the right expectations.

AI will change workplaces. That much is clear. But the idea that 50% of entry-level white-collar jobs will vanish within a few years, as if some switch gets flipped and suddenly every business in the world has seamlessly integrated AI into their messy, idiosyncratic, held-together-with-gaffer-tape operations? That's not how technology adoption has ever worked, and there's no compelling evidence it'll be different this time.

The disruption will be real. My guess is that it'll also be slower, messier, and more uneven than the keynote speeches suggest. Some industries will be hit hard and fast. Others will still be running their operations on an AS/400 and a prayer in 2035. Most will be somewhere in between: cautiously experimenting, selectively adopting, and discovering (as every generation before them has) that the future arrives later than predicted and looks nothing like the brochure.

So, what would you say you do here? For now, probably more than an AI can.
